Networking & Building Connections

Networking isn’t asking for a job—it’s building relationships to learn, grow, and open doors over time. Use this page to follow a simple process and grab ready-to-use templates from CDO.

For a more complete and detailed set of networking resources—including workshop slides, handouts, and templates— please visit our CDO Canvas Networking & Building Connections module.

The Networking Process: Step by Step

  1. Build Your LAMP List

    The LAMP List is a strategic tool that helps you identify and prioritize 40 target organizations for your job or internship search. Instead of randomly applying, the list guides you to focus your networking efforts on companies that align with your interests, values, and opportunities. By filling in the four columns (List • Alumni • Motivation • Posting) you’ll be able to see which organizations have alumni connections, how motivated you are to work there, and whether they’re currently hiring. Once sorted, the LAMP list gives you a clear roadmap of where to start building connections and reaching out.

    • Creating a LAMP List for Strategic Networking

  2. Find Alumni & Connections

    After you finish your LAMP list, the next step is to go through our Intro to Making Connections slide to learn how to identify real people at those organizations and start building relationships. Focus on alumni, professionals, and anyone in your extended network who can share insights.

    • Where to find people

  3. Reach Out & Get a Response

    Send short, clear, personalized messages. Ask for a quick 15–20 minute chat to learn—not for a job. Offer a time window and include a one-line intro about you. For more information (e.g. networking email samples) please go check out the same slide: Intro to Making Connections.

    • [Handout] Networking Outreach Email Examples
    • Networking Email Templates
    • Lettersmith: Writing Support Tool

  4. Follow-Up Strategies

    Why does it matter? Following up shows professionalism, appreciation, and helps you stay memorable.

    • Send a Thank-You Email: Always send within 24 hours. Keep it short, genuine, and specific about what you learned.
    • Connect on LinkedIn: Add a personalized note (e.g., “Great speaking with you today about your career at X company”).
    • Stay in Touch: Share occasional updates, congratulate them on achievements, or send a relevant article.
    • Track Your Outreach: Use a spreadsheet or CareerLink notes to avoid losing track of who you’ve contacted.

  5. Prepare for the Conversation (Informational Interviews)

    Next, go through our Informational Interviews using the TIARA Method slide to have a clear guide of how to interact in interview. An informational interview is an informal conversation with someone working in an area that interests you who will give you information and advice. Guide your chat with the TIARA method (Trends • Insights • Advice • Resources • Assignments). Be curious, take notes, and always send a thank-you.

  6. Show Up Ready (Career Fairs & Conferences)

    Bring a tailored resume, practice your 30-second pitch, and research who you want to meet. Plan follow-ups within 24–48 hours.

Networking FAQ

Common questions students ask when starting networking:

  • Q: How many people should I reach out to?
    A: Aim for quality, not just quantity. Start with 5–10 strong connections, then expand gradually.
  • Q: What if they don’t reply?
    A: Wait 7–10 days, then follow up once politely. If still no response, move on—don’t take it personally.
  • Q: Do I have to ask about jobs directly?
    A: No. Focus on learning, building relationships, and gathering insights. Opportunities often come naturally later.
  • Q: How do I balance networking with coursework?
    A: Dedicate 1–2 hours per week. Small, consistent steps (sending 1–2 outreach messages) are more effective than cramming.

What Makes You Stand Out?

Connections land best when your materials and presence are clear and professional.

  • Resume Snapshot: Be ready to share a concise, action-oriented version that highlights impact (numbers, outcomes) rather than tasks.
  • LinkedIn Presence: Keep your photo professional and your headline clear (e.g., “UX Designer | Bridging Design & Technology”). Add recent projects and skills so people can learn more after meeting you.
  • Professional Presence: Timely replies, clear emails, customized outreach, and prompt thank-you notes.
  • Portfolio / GitHub (as relevant): Show real work—case studies, code, or prototypes to support your story.
